After the Bell, the Work Begins.

Lily Yu | ENGL 281

Some students work twice as hard just to reach the place other students get to start from.

Beginning section

Contrary to the common stock story that all students are provided with equal educational access and that student outcomes reflect their learning ability or work effort, reality shows that education is greatly affected by a student's background and that systematic inequalities in our education system can significantly harm a student's learning outcome and prevent them from reaching their full potential.

Scene 1

Mia is a first-generation immigrant from a low-income family. She is still learning English while trying to keep pace at Lincoln High.

While other students at Lincoln High School head to their AP classes, Mia has to go to a meeting with her school guidance counselor.

Scene 2

The dream sounds possible until someone lists the price of admission.

Mrs. Brown tells Mia that college engineering programs require AP classes, test scores, robotics, and polished extracurriculars, even though Mia's school offers only two AP classes.

Scene 3

After school, the hours that build a resume belong to somebody else.

Clubs meet while Mia babysits her younger siblings, and weekends that could hold tutoring or competitions become dishwashing shifts to help her parents pay rent.

Scene 4

By night, she is still translating, cooking, helping, and caring for her family.

Scene 5

"Some students work hard just to get to the starting line."

Scene 6

For students like Mia, the lack of institutional support can make it difficult to demonstrate their abilities, even when they are highly capable and hardworking.

Final frame

The impact I hope my counterstory will have is to encourage policymakers and educators to think more critically about equity in education and to push for reforms that provide more support for marginalized students.

This could include:

  • Increased funding for under-resourced schools
  • Stronger bilingual and English-language learner programs
  • Improved access to college counseling
  • Policies that recognize students' different starting points rather than assuming all students have equal opportunities

More broadly, I hope my counterstory brings awareness and encourages readers to move beyond blaming individuals for educational struggles and instead consider how institutions and policies contribute to inequality.
